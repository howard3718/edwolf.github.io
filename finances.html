Design

The pack has been designed with an aluminium cooling jacket to extract and deliver heat to and from the cells, whilst enabling the pack to act as a structural component, aiding with packaging. To minimise temperature gradients, the coolant will operate in a counterflow configuration.

The Blade battery cell allows a greater cell-to-pack ratio as the cells can slip into the pack (no need for modules) and the advantages include an increase in the energy density of the pack and reduce need of supplementary materials (such as adhesives) [1]. Additionally, the blade cell are relatively low cost ($100/kWh) and the large surface area allow substantial heat dissipation to reduce risk of thermal runaway. Furthermore, the design of the blades allows the employment of disassembly tabs to allow greater recoverability of cell material and ease of recycling [2].

Results

A three-dimensional thermal analysis showed that under 50 kW of continuous power draw, temperature gradients did not exceed 5oC in the cells or 10oC in the pack. This is important for minimising cell aging and ensure safe operation for the consumer.

Additionally, a lumped capacitance thermal model was developed to study the average thermal behaviour of the battery pack. Simulations showed that in a motorway driving scenario, the cooling system ensures that the battery temperature does not exceed 35oC within different ambient temperatures, which is within the optimal temperature range for a Lithium Iron Phosphate battery to perform.

The peak charging power ranges from 63kW to 70kW at a maximum current of 160A, and 48kW to 55kW at a maximum current of 125A, due to the effect that temperature has on the terminal battery voltage. For initial battery temperatures of 20°C and 30°C, the constant current portion of the charging profile is held between 10% - 40% SoC at 160A and 10% - 60% SoC at 125A. However, at lower and higher temperatures, the current begins to drop much earlier, due to the battery voltage reaching the threshold in a shorter time.

A fast charging time of 10%-80% SoC in under 20 minutes is met at initial battery temperatures of between 13.7°C – 37.4°C for a 160A peak current, and 15.9°C – 35.9°C for a 125A peak current. Beyond these temperatures, fast charging time is significantly slowed. The similarity in results between 160A and 125A is mainly due to the CCCV profile preventing the overcharging of the battery.

During the low, medium, and high-speed sections of the WLTP test cycle, the battery used 2.2kWh of energy over 15km giving the Novus an efficiency of 14.7 kWh/100km or 4.3 miles/kWh. Therefore, the projected WLTP electric-only range of the Novus is 93 miles (150km).

Theoretical Grounding

The Cognitive Control Hypothesis [1] states that the amount of information one’s working memory can hold simultaneously, known as cognitive load, is limited. To be processed effectively and safely, feedback must not place excessive demand upon one’s cognitive load.
Multiple Resource Theory[2] suggests that people have different cognitive resources for processing different types of information, meaning some modalities can be used concurrently without reducing attentional capacity, yet others cannot. Whilst visual and auditory stimuli place demand on the same perceptual resources, haptic cues rely on different resources.
This means haptic cues can be processed alongside visual cues, without exhausting the driver’s cognitive load. Therefore, visual-haptic feedback was selected for the proposed eco-feedback system.
Long-term behaviour retention is achieved using social comparison of short and long-term performance, via the post-driving gamified app. Strategically-timed notifications are delivered to incentivise charging behaviours.

Theoretical Grounding

The active safety systems should be composed of the First Wave of active technologies, being treated as a standard to have on a vehicle, and, since this is a car being designed to launch in 2030 it makes sense to include systems that are part of the Second Wave of active safety, considering this as an investment in the future of the automotive industry and on road safety [3].

The NHTSA, is one of the various Road Safety Assessment Agencies that advocate and try to ensure that passive and active safety are regulated and standardised within vehicle manufacturers. In order to get a 5-star in NHTSA’s rating program a vehicle must possess at least the following active safety systems: Forward Collision Warning, Automatic Emergency Braking  and Lane Departure Warning [4]. Therefore the following active technologies were modelled and included in the Novus 2030: Pre-collision system and Lane Departure Alert with Steering Assist.

The Pre-collision system detects elements, such as pedestrians, bicycles and vehicles ahead. When the possibility of a frontal collision with one of these elements is determined, a warning will be sent out to the driver to take evasive action, if danger persists the vehicle will start braking automatically making use of the BA system.

The Lane Departure Alert with Steering Assist acts when the system determines the vehicle is on a path to unintentionally depart from its lane, sending a signal to the driver and, if no action to change the course of the vehicle is determined, sending corrective steering inputs until the vehicle is within the correct distance from the lane boundaries.
